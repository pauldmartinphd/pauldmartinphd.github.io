Current Research

A central thread in Dr. Martin's current research is software supply-chain security: quantifying, and then reducing, the gap between a software component's nominal version and its true vulnerability exposure — from firmware, through operating-system distributions, to language package managers.

This work grows directly out of Firmware IQ, which identifies the components and versions inside a firmware image and maps them to known CVEs. Dr. Martin's current research stress-tests that premise one layer up, at the operating-system distribution. Enterprise and “stable” Linux distributions maintain soft forks of upstream packages — with incomplete backports, bespoke patches, and out-of-sync LTS trees — so two packages reporting the same upstream version can carry very different real-world exposure. He is quantifying this vulnerability divergence, along with patch lead time, across rolling and enterprise distributions, to ask three questions: whether rolling distributions are in fact more secure against known and unknown CVEs; whether enterprise distributions should be based on rolling LTS packages; and whether distributions should converge on shared soft-forked package versions so that maintenance effort can be pooled rather than duplicated. A planned follow-up extends the same analysis to programming-language package managers. These findings, in turn, motivate the Linux distribution he is currently building, whose patch-management model is designed around what the divergence measurements reveal.

Related and ongoing work includes:

  • Automated binary version extraction for NVD cross-reference based on fuzzy matching
  • Automated analysis of vulnerabilities in containers and virtual appliances
  • Large-scale comparison of the nature and kind of firmware vulnerabilities across and within product classes
  • Comparison of musl and glibc attack surface normalized by code size and severity
  • Performance analysis and optimization of local large language model inference, including forecasting of architecture and parameter-scaling trends across frontier, open-weight, and agentic models
  • Use of large language models for technical research and analysis, and of multimodal large language models for diagnosis of disease progression
